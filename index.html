Mental health is your emotional and psychological wellbeing. It affects how you think, feel, and act each day especially under stress and shapes how you cope with challenges, make decisions, and connect with others. It includes managing emotions, staying mentally clear, building resilience, maintaining self-worth, and supporting healthy relationships and routines like sleep and motivation.


Everyone has mental health, and it can change over time. Simple habits can help, but if struggles start affecting daily life for a while, getting professional support is normal, practical, and worthwhile.

Anxiety

Everyone worries sometimes before a test, a match, a presentation, or a big conversation. Anxiety becomes a problem when the worry feels constant, intense, or hard to control, even when things are “fine.”

Depression

Depression isn’t just “being sad.” It can look like feeling low for a long time, losing interest in things you normally enjoy, or feeling empty or numb. Some teens don’t feel sad—they feel tired, irritated, or disconnected.

Bipolar Disorder

Bipolar disorder involves extreme changes in mood and energy. It usually includes periods of depression and periods of unusually high energy called mania (or a milder form called hypomania). Not everyone who has mood swings has bipolar disorderthis is more intense and lasts longer than typical ups and downs.

Managing Stress

Stress is a normal response to pressure. The goal isn’t to remove it completely, but to spot it early, calm your body, and take one helpful next step.

If you feel unsafe or at risk of harm, seek urgent help. In the UK you can call 999, or Samaritans on 116 123.

Try this for one minute:

  • Breathe in through your nose for 4 seconds
  • Hold for 2 seconds
  • Exhale slowly for 6 seconds
  • Repeat 5 times

Bring attention back to the present by naming:

  • 5 things you can see
  • 4 things you can feel
  • 3 things you can hear
  • 2 things you can smell
  • 1 thing you can taste (or one slow breath)

Write everything on your mind for 2 minutes, uncensored. Then pick one small next step you can do in 10 minutes.

  • Send one message
  • Open the document and write the first line
  • Make a simple plan: “Today I will do X at Y time”

Choose one:

  • Walk outside for 3 minutes
  • Stretch shoulders/neck slowly
  • Drink water and eat something simple
  • Put your phone down and look at one fixed point while breathing

Common stress signals

  • Racing thoughts
  • Tight chest or tense muscles
  • Irritability or low patience
  • Sleep issues

Daily habits that help

  • Consistent sleep/wake time
  • Short walks or movement
  • Regular meals + hydration
  • Less doom-scrolling

When to get support

If stress is affecting daily life for weeks, or you feel overwhelmed most days, it’s worth speaking to someone you trust or a professional.
